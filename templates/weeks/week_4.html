1. Basic Counting Principles

Counting is the backbone of probability and algorithm analysis. Before we can use complex formulas, we must master two simple rules: Sum (Addition) and Product (Multiplication).

AND / THEN

Product Rule

If a procedure can be broken down into a sequence of two independent tasks:

  • Task A has $n_1$ ways.
  • Task B has $n_2$ ways.

Total = $n_1 \times n_2$

Real-World Examples

1. The Wardrobe [Image of shirts and pants combinations]

You have 3 shirts and 2 pairs of pants. How many outfits?

3 × 2 = 6 outfits.

2. The Website

Choose 1 of 5 background colors AND 1 of 4 fonts.

5 × 4 = 20 designs.

3. Binary Code

How many values in 8 bits? Each bit has 2 options (0 or 1).

2 × 2 × ... (8 times) = $2^8$ = 256.

OR / EITHER

Sum Rule

If a task can be done in one of $n_1$ ways OR one of $n_2$ ways, and none of the set of ways overlap:

  • Option A has $n_1$ choices.
  • Option B has $n_2$ choices.

Total = $n_1 + n_2$

Real-World Examples

1. Reading a Book

You want to read 1 book. You have 3 novels and 2 biographies. You can't read both at once.

3 + 2 = 5 choices.

2. Travel Methods

Go to Manila by Bus (3 routes) OR Train (1 route).

3 + 1 = 4 ways.

3. Username Selection

Pick a username that is either a Fruit (5 types) OR a Color (7 types).

5 + 7 = 12 usernames.

3. Factorials ($n!$)

Definition

Factorial is the product of an integer and all the integers below it. It represents the number of ways to arrange $n$ distinct items in a sequence.

$n! = n \times (n-1) \times ... \times 2 \times 1$

Special Case: $0! = 1$

There is exactly one way to arrange zero items: do nothing (an empty set).

4. Combinatorics: Order Matters?

Before we dive into formulas next week, understanding the problem type is key. Ask yourself: Does the order of selection change the outcome?

Order MATTERS

Often called an "Arrangement" or "Code". ABC is different from CBA.

Example 1 Unlock code for a phone (1-2-3 is not 3-2-1).
Example 2 Race results (1st, 2nd, 3rd place).

Order DOESN'T Matter

Often called a "Selection" or "Group". {A, B, C} is the same group as {C, B, A}.

Example 1 Fruit Salad ingredients (Banana + Apple is same as Apple + Banana).
Example 2 Picking 3 friends to go to the mall.

5. Speed Techniques: The "Slot Method" & Shortcuts

In exams and real life, you don't always need complex formulas. These mental models help you solve counting problems in seconds.

1. The Slot Method

Instead of memorizing "Permutation" formulas, simply draw empty slots for each position you need to fill, then write the number of choices inside.

2. Factorial Canceling

When dividing factorials (common in probability), you don't need to calculate the huge numbers. Just expand until you can cancel.

Calculate $\frac{10!}{8!}$

= $\frac{10 \times 9 \times \cancel{8 \times 7 \times ...}}{\cancel{8 \times 7 \times ...}}$

= 10 × 9 = 90

This technique allows you to solve massive factorial divisions in your head!
